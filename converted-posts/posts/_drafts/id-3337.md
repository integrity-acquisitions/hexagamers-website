# Rules Overview

In Citadels, you're racing against other players to build the most prosperous city. Build your eighth district first, have the most points and win the game! Just because you built your eighth district first, though, doesn't mean you're going to win. Keep an eye on everyone, because their actions can hurt you game and your chance at winning.

Thoughts

This is a neat little deck building game. It mixes in different elements including bluffing, secrecy, sabotage and more which keeps players on their toes. It's pretty quick to learn, even though I find it isn't always quick to play--a negative for me, as you have to wait often for your turn to play, which can be a little boring. The two-player variant isn't the best, and we find it's better with at least four players. That said, with all the extras (and all the awesome roles), Citadels is a fun filler game to start off your night. We have the 2000 version, and we like the graphics much better in the 2016 version. The newer version also has more and new roles which is great for new and vet Citadels players.

Playing Citadels is fairly straightforward. There are five different district colours and each card costs something different. Some can be quite pricey, but they can also offer bonuses or extras to help you along. On every turn, you either collect gold to help you build, or choose two cards from the deck and keep one. Whichever action you choose, you can then build a district. Once every player has gone, that round ends and another begins. It sounds simple, right? Here's where Citadel differs. There are 18 different role cards (two sets labelled 1-9), modified to player numbers with one card removed each round to heighten secrecy. At the beginning of each round, every player secretly selects a role, and then they are called in a specific order to play.

Roles range from the assassin, thief and witch, to king, architect and warlord. Each has a different benefit, like choosing additional cards, stealing money, killing other players (who then sit out that round) and getting extra points for districts of a specific colour. It can be difficult to choose just one role per round, as so many of their actions are helpful for your game.

For a simple deck-building game, Citadels likes to throw in curve balls, and it keeps the game fun and challenging. It's definitely a strategy game with a little bit of luck, and it becomes increasingly challenging with more players to keep an eye on. What I like about this game is there isn't a set strategy to win, and you actually have to adapt your strategy as you play. It is quick and easy to learn, and most of the cards are labelled with their bonuses and benefits. There's a cheat sheet that lists every role as well so you can make allowances or wise decisions based on which friend you think has which role.

I also really like that even though a player may have built his eighth district first, this may not mean he won the game. Bonuses are awarded for finishing first or second, having districts of each colour, and so forth.
