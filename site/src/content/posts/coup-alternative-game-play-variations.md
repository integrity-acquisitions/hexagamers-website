Coup is a 15-minute bluffing game where you lie to, steal from, tax, assassinate, and Coup your way to being the last player standing. We went through a phase of playing it at every get-together — and then introducing it to friends who did the same.

After enough plays, any game can start to feel familiar. Before you put it back on the shelf, try some of these variations. We've tested most of these extensively, a few just a handful of times, and flagged one we found online that looked worth trying.

> New to Coup? Start with our [How To Play Coup – Simplified](/coup-how-to-play-simplified/) guide.
> Read our full [Coup Review](/coup-review/).

> **Note:** We won't re-explain the base rules here — these are add-ons and swaps, not replacements.

---

<div class="rule-cards">
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 1</p>
    <p class="rule-title">3 Cards Each</p>
    <p>Deal every player 3 influences instead of 2 to start.</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Games run longer. Bluffing is easier, but so are counter-actions. More interaction per turn. Works especially well for 1v1.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 2</p>
    <p class="rule-title">Reserve Influence</p>
    <p>Everyone gets a third card that sits face-down beside their hand. It only enters play after they lose one of their starting influences.</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: You call bluffs more aggressively because you can absorb a loss. Mid-game your hand dynamics shift when the reserve card comes in. Felt natural — like a delayed third life.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 3</p>
    <p class="rule-title">1 Card Each</p>
    <p>Each player starts with only 1 influence. Optionally narrow the deck to 2 of each character, or leave it full.</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Extremely fast games. The Assassin becomes impossible to bluff with — nobody accepts assassination when it ends their game. High risk, chaotic fun.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 4</p>
    <p class="rule-title">Draft Your Hand</p>
    <p>Deal 3 cards, but players choose which 2 they keep. Return the third to the deck.</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Reduces the "bad hand" frustration (looking at you, double-Contessa start). Didn't change gameplay much but cut down on complaints.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 5</p>
    <p class="rule-title">Remove Influences</p>
    <p>Before dealing, randomly remove 1, 2, or 3 influence cards from the deck entirely.</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Works great for 2–3 and 6-player games. Lying goes up noticeably. When 2 Dukes disappeared in one game, stealing spiked across the board. The Ambassador gains real power when the deck is lean.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 6</p>
    <p class="rule-title">Add Influences</p>
    <p>Requires two copies of the game. Randomly add 1–3 extra influence cards to the deck.</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Lots more bluffing, but bluff-calling drops — players assumed the weird claim must be real. Tax-heavy rounds, more Coups. Chaotic in the best way.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 7</p>
    <p class="rule-title">Buy an Influence</p>
    <p>For 7 coins, a player can draw an additional card from the deck (max 2 total held at once).</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Prolongs games slightly. Bought cards usually get lost fast — opponents Coup you the moment they see you stacking up. Good late-game lifeline or strategy pivot.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 8 ⭐</p>
    <p class="rule-title">Hidden Discard (Our House Rule)</p>
    <p>When you lose an influence, shuffle it back into the deck face-down instead of revealing it.</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Our favourite variation — now our permanent house rule. Nobody knows which card you put back. Bluffing explodes. The advantage of tracking discards disappears, but the final few turns become genuinely tense for everyone.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 9</p>
    <p class="rule-title">Team Alliances</p>
    <p>Split into equal teams (2v2, 3v3, or 2v2v2). Teammates cannot Coup or eliminate each other. All other rules apply.</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Slows the game. Stealing nearly disappears — 6 of 15 cards can block it, so someone almost always does. Fun for the team dynamic but plays very differently.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 10</p>
    <p class="rule-title">Targeted Coup</p>
    <p>When you Coup someone, name which influence you're targeting. If correct, they lose it. If wrong, your 7 coins are wasted.</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Rewards truth-tellers (easy to read and Coup) and punishes consistent liars less. Surviving a Coup attempt is now a real mechanic. We loved this one.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rule-card">
    <p class="rule-num">Variation 11</p>
    <p class="rule-title">Army Exchange</p>
    <p>As your action, choose two players and blindly swap one influence between them. One of those players can be yourself. (Via Board Game Geek.)</p>
    <p class="rule-example">What we noticed: Adds a chaotic twist. You might dump a card you don't want — or accidentally improve someone else's hand. Great information scrambler; adds another layer without breaking the game.</p>
  </div>
</div>

## Wrap Up

Eleven variations, lots of ways to keep Coup fresh. Some are subtle tweaks, some completely change the feel of the game. **Our personal pick: Variation 8 (Hidden Discard)** — it's become a permanent house rule.

Got a variation we didn't cover? Drop it in the comments. If it sounds good, we'll try it and report back.
